Understanding Backflow and Its Risks

Backflow occurs when the normal flow of water is reversed, allowing contaminants to enter the clean water supply. This can happen due to sudden changes in water pressure, often caused by fire hydrant use, pipe bursts, or high demand in the water system. Without proper prevention measures, backflow can introduce harmful substances like fertilizers, household cleaning agents, and sewage into your potable water.

Frequently Asked Questions

A backflow prevention device is a mechanism installed on plumbing systems to prevent contaminated water from flowing back into the clean water supply. This is essential in maintaining safe water quality.

Installing a backflow prevention device is crucial to protect the public water supply from contamination and to comply with local plumbing codes and regulations. It helps ensure the water remains safe for all users.

The responsibility for installing a backflow prevention device typically lies with the property owner. However, it must be installed by a certified plumber or professional who is experienced in backflow prevention to ensure compliance with health and safety standards.

Backflow prevention devices should be tested annually to ensure they are functioning correctly. Some jurisdictions may have specific testing schedules, so it's important to check local regulations.

Signs of a malfunctioning backflow prevention device include discolored water, changes in water pressure, foul odors, or unusual noises in the plumbing system. If you notice these signs, it is important to have the device inspected by a professional.
